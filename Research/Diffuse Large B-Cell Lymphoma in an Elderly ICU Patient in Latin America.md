# Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma in an Elderly ICU Patient in Latin America

## Overview

Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) is the most common aggressive non-Hodgkin lymphoma in adults and is frequently diagnosed in people over 60 years of age. In older adults, especially those in the intensive care unit (ICU) with comorbidities and frailty, management must balance curative intent with treatment tolerance and quality of life while integrating strong supportive care.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

## 1. Standard first-line treatment and when R-CHOP or variants are used

- In fit adults up to about 80 years old, standard first-line therapy is R-CHOP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisone), an anthracycline‑based regimen with curative intent.[^5][^6][^1]
- R-CHOP has been the backbone of DLBCL treatment for more than 20 years and significantly improved event‑free and overall survival compared with CHOP alone, without major added toxicity in elderly patients.[^7][^1][^5]
- Polatuzumab‑R‑CHP (pola‑R‑CHP: polatuzumab, rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, prednisone) showed a modest progression‑free survival benefit over R‑CHOP in high/intermediate‑risk DLBCL aged 18–80; overall survival was similar, and R‑CHOP remains a standard backbone.[^1]
- In very elderly (≥80 years) but not severely comorbid patients, R‑mini‑CHOP (dose‑reduced R‑CHOP) is widely used; 2‑year overall survival around 59–65% has been reported, with more tolerable toxicity.[^8][^9][^1]
- Real‑world analyses show R‑mini‑CHOP is less effective than full‑dose R‑CHOP but still provides meaningful survival in frail older patients; treatment selection should be based on a geriatric/fitness assessment rather than chronological age alone.[^10][^9][^8]
- In double‑ or triple‑hit high‑grade B‑cell lymphomas, dose‑adjusted R‑EPOCH (rituximab, etoposide, prednisone, vincristine, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin) is often preferred in fit, usually younger patients; this regimen is frequently too intensive for very elderly or frail individuals.[^11][^1]
- For truly frail elderly or those with major comorbidities who cannot tolerate anthracyclines, options include further attenuated R‑CHOP‑like regimens, rituximab‑bendamustine, palliative low‑intensity chemoimmunotherapy, or supportive care alone, recognizing lower response rates and shorter progression‑free survival.[^9][^1]

## 2. Contraindications, dose limitations, and reasons to delay or modify chemotherapy in frail older adults

- Anthracyclines (doxorubicin) are relatively contraindicated in patients with significant cardiac dysfunction (reduced left ventricular ejection fraction, recent myocardial infarction, uncontrolled heart failure); alternative regimens or dose reductions may be needed.[^10][^1]
- Poor performance status, severe frailty, malnutrition, or multiple comorbidities (cardiac, renal, hepatic, pulmonary) increase the risk of treatment‑related mortality and drive the choice of reduced‑intensity regimens such as R‑mini‑CHOP or non‑anthracycline combinations.[^4][^1]
- Baseline cytopenias (very low neutrophils, platelets) due to marrow involvement or prior illness may require delays, growth factor support, or transfusions before and during chemotherapy.[^4][^10]
- Renal dysfunction can increase exposure to renally cleared drugs (e.g., cyclophosphamide) and heighten the risk of tumor lysis syndrome (TLS), prompting careful hydration, uric‑acid–lowering prophylaxis, and sometimes dose adjustments.[^12][^13]
- Hepatic impairment may alter metabolism of vincristine, doxorubicin, and other drugs, requiring dose reduction or avoidance to reduce neurotoxicity and cardiotoxicity.[^10][^4]
- Active uncontrolled infection, sepsis, or hemodynamic instability in the ICU are common reasons to delay chemotherapy until the patient is stabilized, as myelosuppression can worsen infection risk and outcomes.[^14][^4]
- Severe functional decline, delirium, or inability to consent may lead teams to reassess goals of care and consider whether curative‑intent chemo is appropriate versus a more palliative focus.[^15][^4]

## 3. ICU‑relevant supportive care for an elderly DLBCL patient

### Oxygen and respiratory support

- ICU teams titrate oxygen and ventilatory support (nasal cannula, high‑flow, non‑invasive ventilation, or intubation) to maintain adequate oxygen saturation while avoiding oxygen toxicity or delirium from hypoxia.[^16][^14]
- For patients with lymphoma‑related lung involvement, infections, or fluid overload, careful monitoring, chest imaging, and diuretics may be required.[^14][^4]

### Infection prevention

- Older adults with lymphoma and chemotherapy are at high risk of febrile neutropenia and life‑threatening infections; prophylactic granulocyte colony‑stimulating factor (G‑CSF) is often used to reduce neutropenic infections and maintain dose intensity.[^4]
- Strict hand hygiene, early removal or meticulous care of invasive lines and catheters, timely cultures, and early broad‑spectrum antibiotics for fever are core ICU practices.[^14][^4]
- Depending on regimen and local policy, prophylaxis against Pneumocystis jirovecii (e.g., trimethoprim‑sulfamethoxazole) and certain viral infections may be considered.[^12][^15]

### Tumor lysis syndrome (TLS) prevention

- TLS risk is elevated in aggressive lymphomas with high tumor burden, high LDH, and renal dysfunction; prevention focuses on vigorous hydration, frequent labs, and uric‑acid–lowering agents.[^13][^12]
- Allopurinol is the most commonly used prophylactic drug, started before chemotherapy and continued through the first cycle; rasburicase is reserved for high‑risk or established TLS.[^13][^12]
- Electrolytes (potassium, phosphate, calcium) and creatinine are checked frequently in the first days after chemotherapy, with rapid correction of abnormalities.[^13]

### Pain control

- Older adults with cancer often experience undertreated pain; assessment should include verbal reports, behavioral cues, and nonverbal signs in delirious or intubated patients.[^4]
- A stepwise approach is used: acetaminophen and non‑opioid adjuvants, then opioids with careful dose titration, monitoring for constipation, respiratory depression, and delirium.[^15][^4]
- Neuropathic pain (e.g., from vincristine) may require gabapentinoids or other adjuvants, balancing benefits against sedation and falls.[^4]

### Mouth care and oral comfort

- Meticulous oral hygiene (soft toothbrush, bland rinses like saline or bicarbonate, avoidance of alcohol‑containing mouthwashes) reduces mucositis, infection, and pain.[^17][^18]
- Regular inspection of the oral cavity by nursing staff, with early referral to dentistry or oral medicine when ulcers, plaques, or thrush appear, is recommended.[^19][^20]

### Hydration and renal protection

- Adequate hydration is central in ICU supportive care and TLS prevention; maintenance or increased IV fluids are used, adjusted for cardiac and renal status.[^13][^14]
- Monitoring of urine output, weight, and daily labs helps detect renal impairment early; nephrotoxic drugs and contrast should be minimized when possible.[^14][^13]

### Nutrition

- Malnutrition is common in older adults with cancer and is associated with worse outcomes, functional decline, and treatment toxicity.[^4]
- Assessment of nutritional risk (weight loss, intake, BMI) should be early and repeated, with dietitian involvement, high‑calorie/high‑protein diets, oral supplements, or enteral feeding as needed.[^15][^4]

### Delirium prevention and management

- Older ICU patients with cancer are at high risk for delirium due to age, infections, polypharmacy, anemia, dehydration, and organ failure.[^14][^4]
- Non‑pharmacologic prevention includes orientation, sleep promotion, glasses/hearing aids, early mobilization, pain control, and minimizing sedatives and anticholinergics.[^14][^4]
- Regular screening with tools such as CAM‑ICU and prompt search for reversible causes (infection, hypoxia, drugs, metabolic issues) are recommended.[^14]

### Pressure injury prevention

- Pressure ulcers are a major complication in bedridden older adults and a quality‑of‑care marker.[^21]
- Key measures include frequent repositioning, specialized mattresses or dynamic support surfaces, moisture control, nutritional optimization, and early treatment of any skin breakdown.[^21]
- Staff education and multidisciplinary prevention protocols can significantly reduce pressure ulcer incidence.[^21]

## 4. Oral complications during cancer treatment and practical measures

- Chemotherapy and radiotherapy commonly cause oral mucositis (inflammation/ulceration of the mucosa), oral infections, xerostomia (dry mouth), dysgeusia (taste changes), and swallowing pain, especially in regimens with high doses or continuous infusions.[^20][^17][^19]
- Mucositis typically appears 7–14 days after chemotherapy when neutropenia peaks, causing severe oral pain, dysphagia, reduced intake, and sometimes infection or sepsis.[^18][^20]

Practical supportive measures:

- Before treatment: dental evaluation, treatment of caries or periodontal disease, removal of sharp edges or ill‑fitting dentures, and patient education on oral care.[^22][^19]
- Daily basic care: soft toothbrush twice daily, non‑abrasive fluoride toothpaste, bland rinses (saline or sodium bicarbonate) several times per day, avoidance of alcohol mouthwashes, tobacco, and alcohol.[^17][^18]
- Diet modifications: soft, moist, lukewarm foods; avoidance of acidic, spicy, rough, or very hot foods and drinks that can worsen mucosal injury.[^23][^19]
- Pain control: topical anesthetic gels or rinses (lidocaine‑containing, as prescribed), systemic analgesics; in severe cases, opioid analgesia or patient‑controlled analgesia may be required.[^19][^20]
- Infection management: prompt treatment of oral candidiasis (e.g., nystatin suspension, systemic azoles when needed) and bacterial infections, guided by culture when possible.[^20][^19]
- Xerostomia management: frequent sips of water, saliva substitutes, sugar‑free gum or lozenges to stimulate saliva when safe, humidified room air, and review of xerogenic medications.[^18][^19]
- Swallowing pain and dysphagia: work with speech‑language therapy or swallowing specialists, adapt food consistency, and consider enteral feeding if oral intake is insufficient.[^22][^18]

## 5. Baseline data a family should gather before oncology decisions

- Pathology report: confirm diagnosis of DLBCL, any mention of double‑hit/triple‑hit features, Ki‑67 proliferation index, CD20 status, cell‑of‑origin subtype (GCB vs non‑GCB), CD5 expression, and other markers.[^24][^7]
- Immunohistochemistry: panel used, expression of B‑cell markers (CD20, CD19, PAX5), germinal center markers (CD10, BCL6), non‑GCB markers (MUM1), proliferation index (Ki‑67), and any adverse markers (CD5, MYC, BCL2, BCL6).[^24][^11]
- Staging information: Ann Arbor stage, presence of bulky disease, extranodal involvement (e.g., bone marrow, CNS, GI), and baseline imaging (PET‑CT or CT) reports.[^2][^3]
- Laboratory data: complete blood count (CBC), comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP), lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), uric acid, electrolytes, albumin; these feed into the International Prognostic Index (IPI).[^3][^24]
- Viral serologies: hepatitis B surface antigen and core antibody, hepatitis C antibody, HIV testing where risk factors exist; these are important before rituximab to prevent viral reactivation.[^11][^1]
- Cardiac assessment: echocardiogram or other assessment of left ventricular ejection fraction and cardiac history (heart failure, coronary disease) to evaluate anthracycline tolerance.[^1][^10]
- Renal and liver function: creatinine, estimated GFR, liver enzymes, bilirubin; these influence dosing and TLS risk.[^12][^13]
- Current medication list: including anticoagulants, antiplatelets, antihypertensives, diabetes medications, psychotropics, and supplements, to anticipate interactions and delirium contributors.[^4]
- Functional and geriatric status: baseline ability to walk, perform activities of daily living, cognitive status, and recent weight loss; geriatric assessment is recommended in older cancer patients to individualize treatment.[^15][^4]

## 6. Key questions for the ICU team and hematology‑oncology team

Examples of high‑yield questions:

- Diagnosis and stage
  - "Can you explain exactly what subtype of lymphoma he has (including any double‑hit or double‑expressor features) and what stage it is?"[^24][^1]
  - "What is his International Prognostic Index (IPI) score, and what does that imply for prognosis with and without treatment?"[^24]

- Treatment options and goals
  - "Is curative‑intent treatment realistic in his case, or are we aiming more for disease control and comfort?"[^1][^4]
  - "If we start treatment, are you considering full‑dose R‑CHOP, R‑mini‑CHOP, or another regimen, and why?"[^9][^1]
  - "What are the expected benefits (chance of remission, survival) versus risks (treatment‑related mortality, ICU complications) given his age and current condition?"[^1][^4]

- Timing and logistics in ICU
  - "Is he stable enough to receive chemotherapy in the ICU now, or do we need to improve certain organ functions first?"[^4][^14]
  - "How will you prevent tumor lysis syndrome and infections when chemotherapy starts?"[^12][^13]

- Supportive care and quality of life
  - "What are you doing to protect his kidneys, heart, and lungs during treatment?"[^13][^14]
  - "How are pain, mouth discomfort, nutrition, and delirium being assessed and managed day to day?"[^18][^4]
  - "What is being done to prevent pressure injuries and maintain mobility as much as possible?"[^21]

- Geriatric and palliative input
  - "Has a geriatric oncology or palliative care team evaluated him to help balance aggressiveness of treatment with comfort and function?"[^15][^4]
  - "If things do not go as hoped, what are the options for comfort‑focused care, and how would those look in the ICU or ward?"[^4]

## 7. Red flags requiring urgent escalation

Families should alert staff immediately if they notice:

- Sudden changes in mental status: new confusion, agitation, inability to recognize family, or unresponsiveness can indicate delirium, stroke, sepsis, or metabolic emergencies.[^14][^4]
- Fever, chills, or shaking, especially in a neutropenic patient, as this may signal sepsis requiring immediate antibiotics.[^4]
- New or rapidly worsening shortness of breath, chest pain, or oxygen requirement, which may reflect pulmonary embolism, pneumonia, heart failure, or disease progression.[^16][^14]
- Marked decrease in urine output, very dark urine, or swelling, suggesting acute kidney injury or TLS.[^12][^13]
- Sudden severe pain (chest, abdomen, bone) or new neurologic symptoms (weakness, difficulty speaking) that could indicate major complications.[^14][^4]
- Rapidly worsening oral pain, inability to swallow, or signs of oral infection (white plaques, bleeding) that block oral intake or suggest systemic infection.[^20][^18]
- New or worsening skin breakdown, especially over bony prominences, as this can progress quickly to severe pressure ulcers and infection.[^21]

## 8. Colombia and Spanish‑language patient resources

- Colombian National Cancer Institute (Instituto Nacional de Cancerología, INC): hosts guidelines and educational materials on adult lymphomas, including DLBCL, mainly for professionals but often accessible as PDFs; these reflect local practice patterns and resource considerations.[^25][^11]
- Revista Colombiana de Cancerología and other Colombian oncology journals publish open‑access articles about DLBCL epidemiology, prognostic factors, and treatment outcomes in Colombian cohorts.[^7][^24]
- Lymphoma Coalition: offers a Spanish‑language DLBCL patient and caregiver toolkit ("La vida con linfoma B difuso de células grandes"), explaining diagnosis, treatment, side‑effects, and questions to ask, tailored to patients and families.[^26]
- International resources in Spanish, such as Mayo Clinic and industry educational pages, provide patient‑friendly explanations of DLBCL symptoms, diagnosis, and treatments.[^2][^3]

These resources can be combined with local information from the treating hospital (e.g., Colombian private clinics or university hospitals) to build a practical family knowledge base.
